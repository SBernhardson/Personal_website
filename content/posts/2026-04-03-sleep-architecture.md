When we talk about recovery in sport, we talk about nutrition, hydration, compression, cold water immersion. Sleep gets mentioned last — or treated as obvious background noise.

That's a mistake. Sleep is the primary driver of physiological adaptation. Everything else is peripheral.

## What Happens During Slow-Wave Sleep

Stages 3 and 4 of non-REM sleep — collectively called slow-wave sleep (SWS) — are when the body does its most critical repair work. Growth hormone secretion peaks during SWS. Protein synthesis accelerates. Neural consolidation of motor patterns occurs. Inflammatory markers from training load are processed and resolved.

Without adequate SWS, none of this happens at the rate athletes need.

## The Athlete Sleep Problem

Elite athletes are paradoxically among the worst sleepers in the population. Early morning training, travel across time zones, pre-competition anxiety, and screen exposure all suppress SWS duration and quality.

Studies consistently show that athletes get less than the recommended 8–10 hours per night, and that the *architecture* of their sleep — not just duration — is often disrupted.

## Practical Interventions

1. **Sleep extension** — even 30–60 minutes of additional sleep opportunity meaningfully improves SWS
2. **Light management** — blue light blocking glasses 90 minutes pre-bed reduce sleep onset latency
3. **Temperature** — core body temperature needs to drop ~1°C for sleep onset; cool sleeping environments (65–68°F) accelerate this
4. **Consistency** — irregular sleep timing is as damaging as short sleep duration

The best recovery protocol in the world won't overcome chronic sleep restriction. Fix sleep first.
