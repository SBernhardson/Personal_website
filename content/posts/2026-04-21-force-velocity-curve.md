The force-velocity (F-V) relationship is one of the most practically useful concepts in strength and conditioning — and one of the most underused.

## The Basics

The F-V curve describes the inverse relationship between the force a muscle can produce and the velocity at which it contracts. At one end: slow, heavy movements producing maximal force. At the other: fast, light movements producing maximal velocity.

Most athletes don't sit at either extreme. They exist somewhere on that continuum — and where they sit determines what kind of training will move the needle most.

## How to Profile Your Athletes

A simple field-based F-V profile requires nothing more than a load-velocity test on the squat or jump squat. By measuring bar velocity across 4–6 loads, you can calculate an athlete's individual F-V slope and identify whether they're force-deficient, velocity-deficient, or optimally balanced.

Tools like the PUSH band or a simple linear encoder make this accessible to virtually any S&C program.

## Training Implications

- **Force-deficient athletes** need heavy strength work — maximal effort lifts in the 85–95% range
- **Velocity-deficient athletes** need speed work — jump variations, plyometrics, and light explosive lifting
- **Balanced athletes** need maintenance across both qualities, with periodization shifts based on competition calendar

The F-V profile should be re-tested every 6–8 weeks to track shifts and adjust programming accordingly.
