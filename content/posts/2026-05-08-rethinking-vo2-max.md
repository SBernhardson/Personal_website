For decades, sports scientists and coaches have treated VO₂ max as a largely fixed, genetically determined ceiling — a number you're born with, and can only nudge upward with years of disciplined training.

New data suggests that view is too rigid.

## What the Research Actually Shows

Recent longitudinal studies tracking elite endurance athletes across multi-year training blocks have found significantly greater plasticity in maximal oxygen uptake than previously assumed. Athletes who modified not just training volume but the *structure* of their high-intensity work saw VO₂ max improvements of 8–14% beyond their previous plateau.

The key variable wasn't how much they trained. It was the interval architecture — specifically, the work-to-rest ratios and the fraction of time spent above 90% of VO₂ max.

## Implications for Practitioners

This doesn't mean VO₂ max is infinitely malleable. Genetic ceilings are real. But the practical takeaway is that many athletes plateau not because they've hit their biological limit, but because their training has stopped providing the right stimulus.

If your athletes have been stuck on their aerobic ceiling for 12+ months, the answer probably isn't more miles. It's smarter high-intensity structure.
